Purpose-driven prospecting

A weak "why" quits after the first rejection. A powerful "why" talks to 10 more people.

Before you train your team to prospect, you must excavate their real why — not the surface answer. The real why lives 3–5 levels down.

The "5 Levels Deep" why exercise

Excavate the real motivation

  • L1

    "Why did you join WFG?"

    Common answer: "To make extra money." — This is not enough to sustain action.

  • L2

    "Why do you need the extra money?"

    Typical: "To pay off debt / help my family."

  • L3

    "What does helping your family look like specifically?"

    Getting specific: "My mom is struggling. I want to buy her a house."

  • L4

    "What would that mean to you?"

    Emotional: "She sacrificed everything for me. She's never had financial security."

  • L5

    "So when things get hard, what are you really fighting for?"

    The real why: "I'm fighting to change the story for my family — forever." THIS is what you write on your mirror.

Newbie

Time & freedom

Usually motivated by escaping the 9-5, being present for kids, or paying off early debt. Anchor them to a vivid future moment, not an income number.

Seasoned

Legacy & leadership

Often motivated by building a team, creating generational wealth, or proving something to themselves. Connect prospecting to building a team that outlasts them.

Recruiting-Side Objections
Raised by someone considering the business opportunity itself
MLM"Isn't WFG just a pyramid scheme / MLM?"
Most common. Stay calm — this is the easiest one to defuse with facts.
"That's one of the most common things I hear — and I totally get why people say it. Let me be direct with you. RFM/WFG is licensed in all 50 states, registered with FINRA, and regulated by state insurance departments. Our parent company is Aegon, nearly 200 years old, managing hundreds of billions of dollars in assets globally.

Now — what does MLM actually mean? It means agents can build teams and earn overrides. You know what else works that way? Keller Williams. State Farm. Every major insurance distribution company in the country. The model isn't the problem. Bad advice from bad agents is the problem — and that exists in every industry.

What specifically made you think pyramid when you heard about us?"
Coaching note: End with a question. Get them talking. Their answer tells you exactly where the real fear is.
Do not say: "We're not an MLM" as your only response. That sounds defensive and weak. Name the model, define it correctly, and compare it to respected companies.
Cost"What does it cost to get licensed / get started / too expensive"
Be upfront and specific — vague answers create suspicion. Use the actual numbers from the Licensing tab.
"Good question — I'd rather be upfront. All-in, getting licensed runs around $700 — background check, pre-licensing course, state exam, fingerprints, and license fee. You don't pay it all at once, and typically helping your first family puts about $1,000 back in your pocket — so you're already ahead before the end of your first month.

Compare that to any other business — franchise, storefront, food truck — we're talking hundreds versus tens of thousands. And once you're operating as an agency owner, everyday expenses can become legal business write-offs, which means potentially saving thousands a year in taxes you'd be paying anyway.

So it's less 'can I afford $700' and more 'can helping one family pay it back?' — and it does, every time. Does that make sense?"
Coaching note: Always pair the dollar number with a comparison to other business startup costs — context makes a small number feel even smaller. See the Licensing tab for the exact line-item breakdown.
Time"I don't have time for a side business"
Usually really means "I don't see how this fits into my life." Show the part-time on-ramp — don't argue about their schedule.
"I hear that constantly, and honestly that's exactly why this is built the way it is — most people start part-time, 8-10 hours a week, around their existing job. Nobody's asking you to quit anything or rearrange your whole life on day one.

The real question is — if there was a way to build something on the side, on your schedule, using time you already have, would that be worth exploring? Because here's what I'd ask back: if nothing changes, what does your schedule look like in five years versus what you actually want it to look like? Sometimes 'no time' is really 'I haven't seen a reason yet to make time.' Is it the time, or is it that you're not sure this is worth your time?"
Coaching note: End by separating "time" from "value" — almost everyone makes time for things they believe in. That's the real conversation.
Online"I just Google WFG and all I see is negative / scam results"
The research objection. This is actually a buying signal — they're doing due diligence, not shutting you out.
"I love that you're doing your research — that tells me you're a serious person and you take this seriously. Let me make that research more useful, because if you just Google us, you're going to end up on Reddit or a forum, and honestly, anyone can post anything there. We have 95,000 licensed agents, and not every experience is the same.

Here's where I'd actually point you. Pull up bbb.org — Better Business Bureau — and search World Financial Group, Johns Creek, GA. A+ rating. That's not bought, that's earned. Then bookmark aegon.com — nearly 200 years old, managing hundreds of billions of dollars globally. Also, tfaconnect.com — that's Transamerica, one of our main carriers. And worldfinancialgroup.com — our brokerage directly.

What I'd love to do is set up a second conversation — and I want you to come in ready to interview us. Bring every question, everything that concerned you, anything you saw. We'll go through it all. And then if you feel good, I'll have a few questions to see if it's a mutual fit. Sound fair?"
Coaching note: Give them the exact URLs. Specificity builds credibility. Vague reassurances don't. Names and links do.
Seasoned closer version: "Let me ask you something. When you research a surgeon before a procedure, do you read the one-star Yelp reviews and cancel the surgery — or do you check their board certifications and hospital affiliations? Google isn't a financial regulator. The BBB is."
Sales"I'm not a salesperson, I'm bad at sales"
A huge, common fear — especially with professionals who pride themselves on NOT being "salesy." Reframe away from selling entirely.
"Honestly, that's actually a good sign — because this isn't built around traditional sales. If anything, the people who struggle most are the ones who come in thinking they need to convince people of something. What actually works is the opposite — sharing something you believe in with people you already know and care about, and letting them decide for themselves.

Think about how you talk about a great doctor, a good restaurant, or a show you binged — you're not 'selling' it, you're just sharing something you think is valuable. That's really all this is. And on top of that, you're trained — you're never doing this alone, especially early on. What part feels more like the sticking point — talking to people, or worrying about being pushy?"
Coaching note: "You're not selling, you're sharing" is the core reframe. Use the "great restaurant" analogy — it's relatable and instantly defuses the fear.
Business"You just want me to recruit / you profit from bringing me in"
This one stings because there's partial truth in it. Lean into the truth and then show the bigger picture.
"You're right that I earn an override if I bring someone on — I'm not going to pretend otherwise. But here's the reality: I earn far more if you stay, succeed, and build a team of your own. That only happens if you're actually helped. My incentive is your success, not your signature.

Every business operates this way. Your manager at work earns more when their team performs. A real estate broker earns when their agents close. The structure isn't the problem — the intention behind it is. And my intention is simple: I want to find people who are genuinely a fit for this and show them what's possible. If that's not you, I'll tell you. I don't want someone who isn't fully bought in — they make everyone's life harder.

So let me ask you honestly — what would need to be true for you to feel like this was worth one real conversation?"
Coaching note: Ending with "I don't want someone who isn't fully bought in" is a power move. It reframes you from the one chasing to the one being selective. Use it with confidence.
Network"My network is small, I don't know wealthy people"
This isn't actually about wealth — it's about access to people who'll take your call. Reframe away from "wealthy" entirely.
"That's actually one of the most common misconceptions about this business — it's not about knowing wealthy people. Everyone needs this, regardless of income — protection, planning, a path forward. What actually matters more is trust, not net worth.

Think about it — do you know at least 10 people who'd take your call if you said 'hey, I'm doing something new, can I get your take'? That's it. That's the starting point. Most of the biggest builders in this business didn't start with wealthy networks — they started with people who trusted them, and it grew from there. So it's less 'who do you know that's rich' and more 'who do you know, period.' Does that change how you're thinking about it?"
Coaching note: Redirect "wealthy" to "trust." This single reframe removes the most common self-disqualifying belief people have.
History"I tried something like this before (MLM) and it didn't work"
Validate the past experience completely — don't get defensive about "this is different." Ask what specifically went wrong first.
"I appreciate you sharing that — and I'm not going to tell you 'this one's different' right off the bat, because I get why that sounds like what everyone says. Can I ask, though — what was it about that experience that didn't work for you? Was it the product, the people, the income model, or just timing in your life? [Listen]

Okay, that's really helpful — because what you're describing is actually a really common pattern, and it's worth understanding why. A lot of those models rely on you buying and reselling inventory, or recruiting being the only way to make money. This is licensed financial services — regulated, real products people actually need regardless of the economy. I'm not asking you to trust me on that — I'm asking if it'd be worth 20 minutes to see how it's actually structured, so you can judge for yourself."
Coaching note: Diagnose before you defend. Their specific bad experience tells you exactly which fear to address — don't guess.
Social"My spouse / friends think this is a scam"
This is about social risk, not the business itself. Normalize the skepticism and give them an easy way to bring people along.
"That's one of the most common things I hear — and I totally get why people say it. Let me be direct with you. RFM/WFG is licensed in all 50 states, registered with FINRA, and regulated by state insurance departments. Our parent company is Aegon, nearly 200 years old, managing hundreds of billions of dollars in assets globally.

Now — what does MLM actually mean? It means agents can build teams and earn overrides. You know what else works that way? Keller Williams. State Farm. Every major insurance distribution company in the country. The model isn't the problem. Bad advice from bad agents is the problem — and that exists in every industry.

What specifically made you think pyramid when you heard about us?"
Coaching note: Never let someone become the messenger for something they don't fully understand yet. Get the skeptic directly into a room or call.
Timeline"How long until I'm actually making money"
Be honest — don't oversell speed, but anchor to the GX 6-month roadmap so it doesn't feel vague.
"Honestly, I'd rather give you a real answer than a hyped one. It depends on effort and consistency, like anything else — but here's the framework: most people who follow the system, get licensed, and actually run the activity see their first real movement within their first one to two months, and the bigger income builds over the first six months as you build a team.

It's not a get-rich-quick thing, and if anyone tells you it is, that's a red flag. What it is is a real business with a real system, and the people who stick to the system tend to see results faster than the people who don't. Would it help to actually see the month-by-month roadmap, so you know exactly what 'on track' looks like?"
Coaching note: Anchor to the actual GX 6-Month Plan in the GX Program tab — specificity beats hype every time, and it sets honest expectations from day one.
Business?"Is this really a business, or just insurance sales with a fancy name"
A legitimacy/identity objection. Lean into ownership, equity, and the team-building model — not just "selling insurance."
"Fair question — and yes, licensed insurance and financial products are part of how income is generated, that's true of any financial professional. But here's the difference: when you build this, you're not just making sales — you're building a business you own. You can build a team, create overrides, and eventually create income that doesn't depend only on your own activity.

Compare that to a traditional sales job — when you stop working, the income stops. Here, the goal is to build something that can outlast your day-to-day involvement. So it's less 'is this insurance sales' and more 'is insurance sales the vehicle, and ownership the destination.' Does that distinction make sense?"
Coaching note: "Vehicle vs. destination" is a strong reframe — the product is the vehicle, business ownership is the destination. Most people are objecting to the vehicle without seeing the destination.
Comp Plan"Another company offered me a higher comp percentage"
Common when a recruit is being courted elsewhere with a flashier number. Don't badmouth the other company by name — let the structure speak for itself.
"That's a fair thing to look into — and I'd actually encourage you to compare carefully, because the headline number isn't the whole story. When another company advertises something like 130% or 150%, the real question is '130% of what?' — most of those numbers are quoted against a different baseline than the standard 100% table, so once you convert it to apples-to-apples, the gap usually disappears or even flips.

The other thing to look at is the override structure above you. With a lot of those plans, every time someone on your team gets promoted, you lose part or all of that override — so the comp looks great on paper until your team actually grows. Here, generational overrides above SMD are permanent and don't get taken away as your people get promoted.

And carrier access matters too — if a company only has one or two product carriers and that relationship changes, your whole pipeline can disappear overnight. We work with 189+ providers and 379+ products, so no single carrier relationship can take us down. A higher number on a smaller, less stable pizza isn't actually a bigger slice. Want me to walk through the real math side by side?"
Coaching note: Always convert competing comp numbers to the same 100% baseline before comparing — that's the single move that defuses this objection. Never badmouth a specific competitor by name; let the structure speak for itself.

Client-Side Objections
Raised by someone considering a financial plan, policy, or strategy
Products"IUL is bad / buy term and invest the difference is better"
This one requires nuance. Don't argue against index funds — that's a losing battle. Reframe the question.
"That's actually a legitimate financial debate — and a good advisor should show you both. Here's the honest truth: buy term and invest the difference is the right answer if the person actually invests the difference. Statistically, most people don't. Discipline is the variable that never shows up in the Reddit math.

IUL isn't for everyone. I'd never put someone in an IUL who doesn't need it. But for someone in a higher tax bracket, who's maxed out their 401k, who wants protection and tax-free retirement income without the market risk — it's one of the best strategies available. A hammer isn't a bad tool because someone used it wrong.

Here's what I do differently from what you read about: I show you both scenarios, side by side, with real numbers. And then you decide. That's what a fiduciary mindset looks like."
Coaching note: The phrase "I show you both scenarios" is critical. It positions you as an advisor, not a salesperson. Use it every time.
Closer move: "Can I ask — has anyone ever shown you what an IUL would look like for your specific situation? Or are you going off what you read?" Most of the time — no. Now you have permission to show them.
Trust"I read bad reviews / people lost money with WFG agents"
Never throw the agent under the bus. But never defend bad advice either. Thread the needle.
"I hear you — and I won't dismiss that. If someone lost money because of bad advice from an agent, that's a real harm and it matters. Here's what I'll say: we have over 95,000 licensed agents. Not every experience is the same — just like not every doctor, attorney, or real estate agent gives the same quality of service. The solution isn't to avoid the industry. It's to find someone you trust.

I'd rather you walk away from me if you don't trust me than buy something because I pressured you. So here's what I'll offer — let's look at the Better Business Bureau together right now. WFG has an A+ rating. You can check every complaint, every response. And then you can decide whether to give me a shot.

What would it take for you to feel confident enough to have one real conversation?"
Coaching note: The BBB offer — "let's look right now" — is powerful because it's transparent and immediate. Pull it up on your phone in the meeting.
Advisor"I already have a financial advisor"
Don't trash their current advisor — that's a red flag to them. Position this as a second opinion, never a replacement.
"That's great — having someone is better than having no one, so I'm not here to replace anyone. Here's what I've found, though: even people with advisors rarely get a true second opinion, because most advisors don't refer you out. Think of it like a second medical opinion — it doesn't mean your doctor is bad, it just means more eyes on something important.

I'm not asking you to switch anything. I'm asking — when's the last time someone actually walked through your numbers with you, just to compare notes? If everything checks out, great, you'll feel even more confident. If there's a gap, wouldn't you want to know?"
Coaching note: "Second opinion," never "replacement." This single word swap removes the loyalty conflict entirely.
Closer move: "Most people have never actually seen their full financial picture on one page — assets, protection, taxes, retirement — side by side. Even if nothing changes, would that be valuable to see?"
Budget"I can't afford to invest anything right now"
Often an "I don't see the value yet" objection wearing a money costume. Don't argue budget — reframe to awareness.
"I hear that a lot, and honestly, that's exactly why this conversation matters — because most people who say that have never actually looked at where their money is going. Sometimes it's not about finding new money, it's about redirecting money you're already spending without realizing it.

That's actually step one of what we do — before we ever talk about putting anything new in, we look at what's already there. If it turns out there's truly nothing to work with right now, I'll tell you that, and we just stay in touch until things change. But most people are surprised what shows up when we actually look. Fair enough to take a look together?"
Coaching note: Never argue "you can afford it." Let the numbers do that work in the actual planning conversation — your job is just to get to the numbers.
Stall"I need to talk to my spouse first / let me think about it"
Rarely a true objection — usually a smokescreen for something unsaid, or a genuine two-person decision. Honor it, but don't let it become a dead end.
"Totally — that makes sense, and honestly I'd actually encourage that, because this should be a decision you both feel good about. Can I ask, though — is there something specific that came up tonight you'd want to run by them, or is it more just wanting to loop them in on something this big? [Listen]

Here's what I'd suggest — let's set up a time, the three of us, so I can walk through it with both of you together. That way you're not stuck trying to explain something you just heard for the first time, and any questions they have, I can answer right there. What night works for you both?"
Coaching note: Always try to convert "I'll ask my spouse" into a three-way follow-up. Letting them explain it alone is how good ideas die in translation.
DIY"Why not just use Robinhood / an app and do it myself"
Respect the DIY instinct — many people who say this are smart and capable. The gap isn't ability, it's bandwidth and blind spots.
"Honestly, if you enjoy that and you're disciplined about it, that's not a bad thing — some of my clients do both. The apps are great for what they're built for: buying and selling investments. What they're not built for is the bigger picture — tax strategy, protection if something happens to you, making sure your accounts actually work together instead of just sitting in separate silos.

Think of it like this: an app can help you build a house, but it won't tell you if you're building it on a bad foundation. My job isn't to compete with the app — it's to make sure everything you're doing, including what's in the app, is actually working toward the same goal. Would it be useful to see how everything fits together?"
Coaching note: Never frame this as "apps are bad." Frame it as "apps are a piece — I look at the whole picture."
Roth"I'll just do a Roth or Backdoor Roth"
Good for clients already maxing a Roth/IRA. Don't disparage the Roth — show its ceiling, then show what fills the gap above it.
"Love it — a Roth is a great foundation, and I'd never talk anyone out of one. A couple of things worth knowing, though. If you have any pre-tax IRA money sitting around, the pro-rata rule can make a backdoor Roth conversion less clean than it looks — it could trigger a bigger tax bill than you'd expect.

Also — $7,000 a year is the ceiling. If you're serious about building meaningful tax-free retirement income, that cap gets hit fast. An IUL has no contribution limits, no RMDs, no market losses, and the same tax-free treatment on the way out. Wealthy people don't use it instead of a Roth — they use it because $7,000 a year isn't enough.

So it's not really Roth vs. IUL — it's whether $7,000 a year covers everything you're trying to build. Does it?"
Coaching note: Never frame this as "Roth bad, IUL better." Frame it as "Roth is great — here's the ceiling, and here's what fills the gap above it."
Fees"WFG products have high fees / MERs of 2–4%"
This is a Canadian-specific critique (MERs / mutual funds). In the US context, redirect to the value of access and planning.
"Fees are a real conversation and I appreciate you bringing it up — because anyone who dismisses that question doesn't have your best interest in mind. Here's what I'll say: yes, some products have higher costs than a Vanguard index fund. And here's what that buys you: a licensed professional who is accountable, who meets with you in person, who helps you navigate the whole picture — income protection, tax strategy, retirement — not just one account.

The real question isn't the fee. It's what you get for it, and whether you'd actually execute a low-fee strategy on your own. Most people don't. That gap — between what they should do and what they actually do — costs far more than any MER.

We work with 200+ carriers. I don't have one shelf. My job is to find what's right for you — even if that means I recommend something outside of WFG. That's how I sleep at night."
Coaching note: "That's how I sleep at night" is a closer line — use it only when the rapport is there. It signals integrity without sounding rehearsed.
Need"I'm young and healthy, why do I need life insurance"
Reframe from "death benefit" to "cheapest it will ever be" plus living benefits — especially relevant heading into an IUL conversation.
"Honestly, that's the best possible time to have this conversation — because being young and healthy is exactly what makes this affordable. The cost of coverage is based on your age and health today, not what they'll be in 10 or 20 years. Lock it in now, and you're protected at today's rates for life, even if your health changes later.

And depending on how it's structured, this isn't just about what happens if something happens to you — some of these strategies build cash value you can actually use while you're alive, for things like a future home, your kids' education, or supplementing retirement. So really, the question isn't 'why now' — it's 'why would I want to pay more for the same thing later?'"
Coaching note: This is one of the easiest "math" objections — youth equals the lowest price it will ever be. Lead with that fact before anything else.
401k"My 401k through work is already enough"
Don't compete with their 401k — most people are emotionally attached to "I'm already doing something." Build on it, don't attack it.
"That's great — seriously, having something set up through work already puts you ahead of a lot of people, so don't discount that. Here's the thing though: a 401k is one tool, but it's usually just one type of account, with one type of tax treatment, and limited investment choices set by your employer.

The question I'd ask is — do you know what percentage of your retirement income will be taxable later, based on what you're contributing now? Most people have never actually run that number. That's really all we'd be looking at — not replacing what you have, just making sure it's working as hard as it can alongside everything else. Worth 20 minutes to see where you stand?"
Coaching note: "One tool" framing works because it's true and non-threatening — you're adding to their toolbox, not criticizing their hammer.
Annuities"Annuities are a scam, I read they're terrible"
Same playbook as the IUL objection — don't argue the internet take. Differentiate old products from current strategies, and ask what they're actually picturing.
"Yeah, I get why people say that — and honestly, some of the older annuities from years ago deserved that reputation. High fees, long surrender periods, confusing terms. But 'annuity' is actually a huge category — it's like saying 'all cars are bad' because you had one bad experience with one model from 1995.

The strategies we look at today are built very differently — things like principal protection with upside tied to the market, or strategies specifically designed to multiply money for long-term care. Can I ask — when you picture an 'annuity,' what specifically comes to mind? [Listen] Got it — that's actually really different from what I'd be showing you. Want me to show you the difference side by side?"
Coaching note: Get them to define what "annuity" means to them first. 90% of the time they're picturing one specific bad product, not the category.
Bank"How is this different from my bank's advisor"
Most bank advisors are limited to in-house products. Position around access and independence — don't bash the bank.
"Good question — and the honest answer is, it really depends on the person, but here's the structural difference. Most bank advisors can really only offer you the bank's own products — that's what they're trained on and incentivized to sell. I work with over 200 different carriers, so I'm not limited to one shelf. That means if something doesn't fit you, I'm not stuck trying to make it fit anyway.

I'd compare it to going to a store that only sells one brand versus going somewhere that can actually shop around for you. Doesn't mean the bank's option is bad — just means you might not be seeing everything that's out there. Want me to show you what else is available for what you're trying to do?"
Coaching note: "200+ carriers" is a real differentiator — repeat it. It's concrete and verifiable, unlike vague claims of "personalized service."
