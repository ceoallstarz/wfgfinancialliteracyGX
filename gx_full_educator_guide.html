Why GX exists — the real reason

The problem GX was built to solve

Before GX, every person on your team was chasing a different goal. One person wanted their MD title. Another was running for SMD. Someone else was chasing a ring. That meant as their SMD, you were managing three different scorecards, three different timelines, three different conversations every time you called them.

And here's what Alicia saw in the data: people would run hard for a promotion — 30, 60 days of intensity — then completely stop. The promotion was a sprint. Business is a marathon. The moment they got the title, the urgency disappeared.

"My dad hates promotions not because titles are bad, but because they make people run one time. A business doesn't run on when you're excited. McDonald's doesn't have 'SMD McDonald's.' They just make money every day."
— Alicia Nguyen, SEVC

GX solves this by giving everyone — regardless of title, regardless of how long they've been in — one shared language, one shared scoreboard, one shared monthly standard. You're not managing their promotion. You're running the same race as them.

What GX actually does for your team
1
Same standard for everyone — TA, MD, SMD. No hierarchy of goals.
2
Monthly reset forces action every 30 days, not just during "runs."
3
Double factor: 3 people trying = 6+ recruits in your baseshop, even if only 1 hits it.
4
Points + recruits in one target keeps both production and building happening simultaneously.
5
Flexible counting — combine months, catch up, go deep or wide. No sandbagging needed.
6
Promotions still happen — organically, from doing GX consistently. You stop chasing titles and start building a real business.

The mindset shift — before anything else

This is the hardest part. Get this right first.

Most people who fail at GX don't fail because they lack skill. They fail because they carry the wrong operating system into the program. Here are the five mindset shifts that separate people who hit GX every month from those who try once and stop.

From promotion-driven to system-driven

Stop thinking "I need to hit SMD." Start thinking "I need to run 3/15K this month." Every promotion you want — MD, SMD, EMD, CEO — comes naturally when you're consistent at GX. The promotion is a byproduct, not the target. When you chase the title, you sprint then stop. When you run the system, you build a real business.

From event-based to habit-based

Your energy cannot be your engine. Some days you're fired up. Some days you're not. The business doesn't care. McDonald's doesn't have an off-day. Your job is to build recruiting habits so strong that you do the work whether you feel like it or not. Three recruits per month is one per week. That's a manageable target you can hit on your worst week.

From managing people to running your own race

Stop spending your energy on why people aren't doing what they said. The answer is always the same: go recruit more. If three people say no to GX, recruit three more and ask them. You're not looking for everyone to say yes — you're looking for the ones who will. Stop focusing on who isn't running and start recruiting until you find who is.

"Why do you always focus on the people that don't do it? Can you just keep recruiting until you find someone that wants to run with you?"
— Alicia Nguyen

From fear of rejection to volume thinking

When you book one appointment and get a no, it feels like your world ended. When you book ten appointments and get two nos, you feel momentum from the eight follow-ups. The math of this business rewards volume. Lillian doesn't pick and choose who she talks to. She talks to everyone. She doesn't assume. She doesn't prejudge. She lets the numbers work for her.

From "I missed it" to "I'm still running"

Brad Hamada had 4 recruits in a full year — then became one of the most explosive builders in the hierarchy. Phoebe tried for nearly a year before hitting her first GX1. Kevin missed consistently for years. The data shows clearly: the only people who never get there are the ones who stop trying. Alicia's rule is simple — every time you miss, next month you go again. The muscle grows with reps, not with wins.

The obsession framework — from Lillian Umera, GX5

Lillian has a full-time healthcare job, two kids, and hit GX5 faster than almost anyone. Her framework isn't complicated. It's five areas of obsession practiced every single day:

1
Obsessed with prospecting. She goes out and meets people every day. Not when she feels like it. Every day. She doesn't pick or choose — she talks to everyone because she genuinely doesn't know who needs this.
2
Obsessed with follow-up. She goes back months later to people who said no — with full energy, no grudge, fresh perspective. Circumstances change. Her follow-up list never dies.
3
Obsessed with recruiting and taprooting. The moment someone says yes, she contacts her SMD immediately and introduces the new person. Same day. Same week. That same month's taproot is what builds legs — and legs are what build GX.
4
Obsessed with Fast Start School. Getting new recruits into Fast Start school as fast as possible is her #1 priority after someone joins. She went from 1–2 people at Fast Start to 5–6 at a time because of this habit.
5
Obsessed with SMD communication. She texts her SMD morning and afternoon. "What's next? What can I do?" She never waits to be managed — she initiates.
Months 1–2 — build yourself first
Phase 1 You are the proof of concept

Why you must go first

Here's the truth about duplication: you cannot teach what you haven't done. If you're trying to get your team running GX while you haven't hit it yourself, you have no authority in that conversation. No credibility. They can feel it.

When you hit GX1 personally and then say "I did it, here's how, let's do it together" — that's a completely different energy. That's leadership by example. That's what makes people believe it's possible for them.

"As a full-time SMD, if you cannot hit 3/15K, then what are you doing every day? You should wake up thinking about who you're going to recruit today."
— Alicia Nguyen (to full-time SMDs in her hierarchy)
What you actually do — month 1

The month 1 playbook

1
Build your list immediately. Before you do anything else, write down 25–30 names. Not your best leads — everyone. College friends, coworkers, neighbors, former colleagues, family friends. The list is your inventory. Without it, you have no business.
2
Contact 10 people every day. Not just text — call. The phone is the engine. If you're doing calls, things happen. If you're just messaging, things crawl. Aim for 3–5 booked appointments per week from those 10 contacts.
3
Run a BPM or VGO every week. Your Saturday morning is a super VPM. Every week. This is where your prospects see the presentation, your new recruits edify you, and your team gets in front of fired-up leadership. Never skip it.
4
Recruit 3, taproot at least 1 same month. The moment someone joins, introduce them to your upline immediately. Help them get their first client or recruit that same month. This is what creates a leg — and a leg is what drives your GX points.
5
Submit 15K+ points. Don't worry too much about points chasing — when you taproot your recruits and they join-own-share, points follow naturally. Focus on people. Points are the byproduct of people getting started.
6
Get yourself to Fast Start School. If you haven't been recently, go. And qualify every single recruit you bring in to attend too. Fast Start school is the launchpad — it dramatically increases the speed at which new people get their first result.
What you actually do — month 2

Month 2: you're now GX2 if you hit it again

1
Hit GX1 again personally. Do not stop recruiting while you start coaching. The moment you stop personal recruiting, your team loses its pace-setter. You need to be running alongside them, not behind a desk managing them.
2
Introduce GX language to everyone on your team. In every conversation with every recruit, the question is: "Are you running GX with me this month? Here's what it means — 3 recruits, 15K points. I'll help you get there." Not a lecture. One sentence. Ask the question.
3
Find your first willing GX partner. Out of everyone you've recruited so far, one person is going to say yes when you ask them to run GX. That person gets your full attention this month. Help them recruit their 3. Make 3-way calls. Show up to their VGO. Be in the trenches with them.
4
Post your own numbers daily. In your team group chat, every day, post: calls made, appointments booked, recruits. You are setting the culture. When they see their SMD posting daily, some of them will start posting too. That's how accountability spreads — not through mandates, through modeling.

Month 3 — 3 people doing GX1
Phase 2 You + 2 others

Why 3 matters — the tipping point

Three people doing GX1 is not just a number. It's the proof that the system is duplicatable. Once you have three people who have personally hit GX1, you now have three people who can teach others how to do it. That's when GX stops being your program and starts being the team's program.

It also changes the energy in every team call. Instead of you explaining what GX is, you have two other people sharing their experience of hitting it. Peer testimony is ten times more powerful than SMD instruction.

The month-start ritual — do this on day 1 of every month

The 10-minute launch call

On the first business day of every month, book a 10-minute call with every person in your base. Not a group call — individual. The agenda is always the same:

1
"Who are you planning to call this week?" Don't accept "I'll figure it out." If they have names, great. If not, open their phone with them and identify 5 people right now.
2
Make live calls together. Right there on the spot, do a 3-way call to one of their contacts while you're on the phone. Nothing teaches like doing. Nothing builds confidence like making the call and having it go well.
3
Set their GX declaration for the month. "Tell me exactly how many recruits you're committing to this month." Get a number. Write it down. You'll come back to it weekly.
4
Confirm their Fast Start and BPM schedule. Are they attending this Saturday's VPM? Are their recruits attending? Are any of them qualified for Fast Start school?
The accountability system that actually works

Daily numbers — the culture you're building

1
Daily team post in your group chat: Every person in your base posts their numbers each evening — calls made, appointments booked, recruits. Not as surveillance. As team culture. When everyone sees each other's numbers, something shifts: people want to contribute to the board.
2
You respond to every post. Not a long coaching session — just acknowledgment. "Let's go." "Who's the recruit? Get me on a 3-way." "Two more appointments this week and you're there." Being seen matters more than you know.
3
Mid-month check: who's close to GX? Around day 15, look at everyone's numbers. If someone has 2 recruits, you get personally involved. You make calls with them. You show up to their VGO. You find their third recruit with them. This is the difference between an SMD and a manager.
4
End-of-month recognition: Anyone who hits GX1 gets recognized publicly — in the group chat, on the weekly call, on Telegram. By name. With context. "Sarah hit GX1 this month — her first. She made calls every single day this week to close it." Recognition repeated becomes culture.
What to say when someone misses
"That's okay. You tried. The muscle takes time. Look at your numbers — you had 2 recruits. That means next month we just need to find one more. What would your life look like if you did this consistently for 12 months straight? That's what we're building. Let's go again."
— How to respond when your team doesn't hit GX

The worst thing you can do when someone misses is make them feel ashamed. The second worst is making it feel optional. Hold the standard warmly. Celebrate the attempt. Redirect immediately to next month.

Months 4–5 — teaching duplication
Phase 3 Your GX people now recruit their 3

The shift that makes everything compound

This is where most SMDs make a critical mistake: they stop recruiting personally and start "managing" their team. The moment you do that, growth stalls. Your job in months 4 and 5 is to do two things at the same time — run your own GX and teach your existing GX people how to run GX with their recruits.

Think of it this way: you have 3 people doing GX1. If each of those 3 people recruits 3 new people and helps 1 of them hit GX1, you now have 6 people doing GX. That's the multiplication. And it only works if your GX people know how to teach the same thing you taught them.

What to teach your GX1 people to do with their recruits

Passing down the system exactly

1
Teach them the month-start call. Your GX1 people should now be doing the same 10-minute calls with their base that you do with yours. "Who are you calling this week? Let's make a call together right now." You model it with them, they replicate it with their recruits.
2
Teach them how to run a VGO. Every new recruit your GX1 person brings in needs a Virtual Grand Opening within their first 2 weeks. Teach your GX1 people to schedule it, build the guest list, edify their new recruit, and close the room. You join the first few. Then they run it themselves.
3
Teach them the taproot conversation. The day someone joins their team, they should be on a 3-way call with that new recruit and you. "Hey, I want to introduce you to my SMD." That introduction builds trust, establishes the system, and massively increases the new recruit's likelihood of staying active.
4
Teach them to recognize their people. When one of their recruits hits a milestone — first appointment, first client, first GX1 attempt — they should be calling it out in the team chat. Culture is built one recognition at a time.

Month 6 — 9 people doing GX1
Phase 4 Your baseshop is a GX machine

What 9 GX people actually means

Nine people consistently hitting GX1 is not a team anymore. It's a system. It's a culture. The difference is that when you have a team, you're motivating people. When you have a system, the system motivates the people — and you're just making sure the system keeps running.

At this point, your job as SMD changes. You're no longer the primary recruiter for everyone. You're the pace-setter, the recognizer, the connector, and the trainer of trainers. Your GX veterans — the people who have been doing this for 4–5 months — are now capable of bringing new people all the way to GX1 without you in every conversation.

The 9-person team structure
1
You (GX6+). Still running GX personally every month. Never stopped. The team sees this and it matters every single time.
2
2 GX veterans (from month 1–2). These are your most experienced GX runners. By now they understand the system deeply enough to explain it to new people. They're becoming trainers. Invest in developing them as leaders.
3
3 mid-level GX runners (from month 3–4). Consistent for 2–3 months. They've hit it, missed it, come back. They're building the muscle. Their recruit base is growing. Some of their recruits are now running for GX themselves.
4
3 new GX1 qualifiers (first time this month). Someone who just hit GX1 for the first time is the most powerful person in your next team call. Let them speak. Let them share exactly what they did. Fresh testimony creates belief faster than any training.
What to do with your first-time GX1 qualifiers

First-time GX1 is a cultural moment — treat it that way

1
Recognize them publicly and specifically. Don't just say "congrats." Say: "Maria hit GX1 this month — her first. She had 2 recruits by week 2 and spent week 3 helping one of them get their first client to close the points. She did not quit when it got hard at day 20." That story is what others model.

Ask them to speak on the next team call. Two minutes. What did you do? What was hard? What made the difference? Peer voice from a brand-new GX qualifier is the best recruiting tool you have for people still on the fence.
3
Immediately challenge them for month 7. "You just proved you can do it once. Now let's make it twice. Same system. Who are three people you're calling this week?" Don't let the momentum of month 6 stop at the celebration.

The duplication principle — why GX spreads

Duplication is not about copying. It's about simplicity.

Something duplicates when a normal person — not a superstar, not a natural salesperson, not someone with a massive warm market — can look at it and say "I can do that." 3 recruits and 15,000 points per month is that thing. It's specific enough to be a real target. Simple enough that anyone can explain it in 30 seconds.

"Is it normal for one person to recruit 100,000 people a year? No. But what makes it doable is a system. Normal people can build a business through a predictable system."
— Alicia Nguyen
The 4 laws of GX duplication

Law 1 — you must do it before you can teach it

You cannot duplicate what you haven't mastered. If you've never hit GX1 personally, your conversation about GX with your team will always feel theoretical. Once you've hit it, you have a real story. "Here's exactly what I did. Week 1 I called these people. Week 2 someone said yes. Week 3 I taprooted them." That specificity is what creates belief.

Law 2 — teach the behavior, not just the result

Most SMDs teach their team to "get GX1." But the people who consistently hit it are taught to "make 10 calls a day, book 3 appointments a week, taproot within the same month." Behavior is what you duplicate. Results follow. When your team knows the specific daily actions, they don't need motivation — they have a checklist.

Law 3 — peer testimony outperforms SMD instruction

When you stand up on a team call and explain GX, people respect you but think "you're the SMD, of course you can do it." When someone at the same level as your newest recruit stands up and says "I hit GX1 last month — here's what I did" — that's a different conversation. Build a culture where your GX qualifiers always share their story immediately after hitting it.

Law 4 — the system must be simpler one level down

When you teach your GX1 people how to develop their team, give them a simpler version of what you do. Not 10 things — 3 things. "Call your base on day 1 of the month. Make 3-way calls. Recognize publicly." The more layers the system has, the harder it is to pass down. Simplicity is what survives four levels deep.

The conversation that creates GX partners

How to ask someone to run GX with you

This conversation works after a BPM or after someone has just joined. It should take about 90 seconds:

"I want to share something with you. I'm running a program called GX this month. The goal is simple — 3 recruits and 15,000 points. I've been doing it every month and it's changed how I think about this business. I'm looking for 2 or 3 people to run it with me — people I can support, make 3-way calls with, and celebrate wins with. You in?"

If they say yes: "Perfect. Here's what we do this week..." and immediately get into the month-start call rhythm. If they say no: that's fine. Recruit 3 more people and ask again. The conversation doesn't change — you just find the person who says yes.

The SMD operating system — what you do every week

Your job is not to motivate people. Your job is to run the system.

Motivated people come and go. Systematized teams keep running. The difference between an SMD who builds and one who stalls is whether they have a repeatable weekly operating rhythm that runs regardless of their mood, their team's mood, or what's happening in the world.

Weekly operating rhythm

Every week, the same structure

Mon
Individual check-ins. Quick text or call to each base member. "How many calls did you make last week? Who are you talking to this week?" Not a coaching session — a pulse check. 5 minutes per person.
Wed
3-way call sessions. Block 2 hours where you're available to jump on 3-way calls with your team members. This is where you add the most value — showing up in their conversations, helping them close, helping them handle objections.
Sat
Super BPM/VGO. Every Saturday without exception. Everyone's newest prospect plugs into this presentation. Your team edifies each other. GX qualifiers from the week get recognized. New recruits see the community they're joining.
EOM
End-of-month recognition. Public, specific, story-based recognition for every GX qualifier. Post in group chat, announce on the team call, send to Telegram. Make it a moment. People repeat what gets recognized.
Day 1
Month-start calls. First business day of every month — 10-minute individual call with every base member. Set their GX declaration. Make live calls. Lock in their weekly targets.

The rule when your team isn't running

You can't change people — but you can change your people

If after two months someone still isn't trying for GX — not missing it, not trying — they are showing you what they want right now. That's okay. But your energy belongs with the people who are running. Stop trying to push people who aren't moving and instead go recruit someone new who is hungry.

"You can't change your people, but you can change your people. Go recruit someone who wants it."
— Team Revolution leadership

This is not giving up on people. It's understanding that your energy spent recruiting three new hungry people will always outperform your energy spent trying to re-motivate one reluctant person. Keep the door open. Go find the people who are ready.
